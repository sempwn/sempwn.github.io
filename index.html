About

I am currently a senior scientist at the British Columbia Centre for Disease Control and an adjunct professor at Simon Fraser University in the Faculty of Health Sciences. Previously, I was a postdoctoral research fellow at the University of British Columbia, the British Columbia Centre for Disease Control and the BC Ministry of Mental Health and Addictions. I use an array of approaches from Bayesian statistics and mathematical modelling.

I also completed a postdoctoral fellowship at the University of Warwick modelling the elimination of lympahtic filariasis as part of the neglected tropical disease modelling consortium.

I completed a PhD in the modelling of pattern formation in ecology as part of the University of Warwick Centre for Complexity Science.

I am passionate about using a wide-variety of analytic and statistical tools to get the most insight from data available; from data-mining to inference and model-based prediction.

I am always interested in new opportunities for collaboration. My contact details are below.

Statistical Methodologies

Data in ecology and epidemiology is often sparse and noisy. The goal is to be able to perform model fitting based on these data combined with expert knowledge. Using Bayesian methodologies allows unknowns to be taken into account and to avoid over-fitting. My research goals are to leverage the full information from data in order to better inform modelling work.

Lymphatic Filariasis

As part of the Neglected Tropical Diseases modelling consortium, I investigated the elimination of lymphatic filariasis as a global public health problem. Lymphatic filariasis or elephantiasis is a mosquito-borne parasitic infection that can cause swelling of the limbs and testes. Currently 790 million people are at risk of the disease and 68 million are currently infected with a further 20 million suffering from chronic morbidity.

Online Modelling Tools

Modelling work can provide compelling evidence to policy-makers for the right decisions to make when faced with challenges such as the control of an epidemic. Modelling tools, where a non-modelling expert may enter certain scenarios and change some assumptions of the model allow users to interact directly with these tools and provide a way of delivering relevant analysis directly to those who need it most.

Pattern Formation

Complex systems in ecology and epidemiology can often produce striking spatial patterns from often simple underlying physical processes. These emergent behaviours can have a number of fascinating implications on the dynamics and stability of such systems. I have tried to understand the implications of some of these phenomena through my research in spatial ecology.
